### 1. The Rule of Thirds & Grids

Composition begins with structure. Use a system to organize elements. The **Rule of Thirds**—placing key focal points at the intersections of a 3x3 grid—creates more tension and interest than simple centering, leading to a more dynamic layout.

### 2. Hierarchy through Scale

If everything is bold, nothing is bold. Use **Scale** to tell the viewer's eye where to look first. A massive headline paired with a smaller sub-headline and even smaller body text creates a clear "path" for the brain to follow, ensuring the most important information is processed first.

### 3. Leave adequate Whitespace (Negative Space)

Whitespace is not "empty" space; it is a tool. It gives the design room to breathe and prevents cognitive overload. Proper use of whitespace focuses the viewer's attention on the subject and gives the design a premium, "stylish" feel.

### 4. Typographic Contrast

Pairing fonts is an art of opposites. Look for **Contrast** rather than similarity (e.g., a bold Sans-Serif for headers with a classic Serif for body text). Avoid using more than 2–3 typefaces in a single project to maintain professional cohesion.

### 5. Alignment for Clarity

Every element on a page should have a visual connection to something else. Whether it’s left, right, or center aligned, consistent **Alignment** eliminates randomness and makes a layout feel "functional" and intentional rather than accidental.

### 6. The 60-30-10 Color Rule

To create balanced, "loved" color palettes, suggest the **60-30-10 Rule**: 60% a dominant neutral color, 30% a secondary color, and 10% an accent color for "calls to action." This ensures the design is vibrant without being overwhelming.

### 7. Proximity and Grouping

Related items should be placed close together. By managing **Proximity**, you create visual groups that help the user categorize information quickly. 

### 8. Focal Point (Emphasis)

Every design needs a "hero." Whether it’s a striking image, a bold splash of color, or a large piece of type, the **Focal Point** is the anchor of the composition. Without it, the eye wanders and the viewer loses interest.

### 9. Repetition and Consistency

Repetition creates rhythm and strengthens a brand's identity. Using the same bullet styles, color accents, or font weights across multiple pages (in print or web) builds a sense of **Consistency** that makes the user feel "safe" and familiar with the interface.

### 10. Color Meaning & Accessibility

Design isn't just about what looks good; it's about how it works. Colors carry **Psychological weight** (e.g., blue for trust, red for urgency) and must have enough contrast to be readable by everyone, including those with visual impairments.
