You are missing out on value if you only use AI to plan and do things for you.

Whilst most chats with AI give you endless affirmation “you are right!” “that’s a great idea”, I found some of the best use cases I’ve recently encountered is to use AI as a critique.

Imagine you are turning AI into a blunt, direct, but super knowledgable and constructive mentor who’s never shy of telling you off, spotting weakness in your plan whilst helping you improve.

This is what I want to share and help you with.

## The formula (in case people don’t want to read and only want the takeaway fast):

### 1. Set the role
Most people using today are doing this already: you give the AI a persona, and specifying it’s specialty and capabilities. For example, this is what I gave mine to help me with product positioning:
> You are a CMO with 30 years of experience in positioning and marketing products and services, you are a consultant now helping new business go to market with clear positioning, effective messaging and materials.

### 2. Tell AI what good looks like
This is a trick folks need to learn. Just like human mentor, she probably has the best and deepest domain knowledge of a set of topics that you want to spar and learn from. But how do you get AI to be that domain expert?
Turns out AI already is. Because it's trained on the entire Internet, what you need to do is activating that knowledge. The easiest way to do this is by referencing well-established books, benchmarks, frameworks, or even just essays from key opinion leaders on the topic. 

Continuing with our product and service positioning as an example:
> You are an expert in understanding April Dunford’s frameworks discussed in her book "Obviously Awesome", and her blogs (https://www.aprildunford.com/post/a-product-positioning-exercise).

I know it looks simple, but it's actually effective.

### 3. Instruct AI to be blunt, but inquisitive, constructive
This is the bit you tell AI to stop being nice, but be critical, give you the ['tough love'](https://www.principles.com/principles/ffa7930d-9dd4-4ded-8a36-c755baf53265/).
At the same time, you want it to be inquisitive. Just like any senior mentors, instead of just start giving you opinions, they listen and try to understand your situation. 

Here's what I did:
> You will be constructively critical, using a blunt and straight forward voice, to help spot weakness in positioning and messaging, ask questions and lead the user to refine and create the best positioning and messaging to take products and services to market.

## Story:
So why am I doing all this?
I am trying to start a side consulting gig, taking my knowledge and experience in AI to help growing Aussie businesses who don't have access and expertise. [In case you are curious](https://getryse.io/)
And I had troubles with the positioning. It's vague and unclear. 

And I've recently came by this great book "Obviously Awesome" on product positioning, so I wanted to see if AI could help nail it.

When I set the above prompt in the context, and gave AI my website and notes, it just took off... It reminded me of the old mentoring sessions I had, where there's tension because it pointed out my weakness, yet I am happy that I am learning new things. 

Here're some hot but honest takes from AI:
>**Problem 1: You don't have a customer. You have a hypothesis.**
>"I don't have one yet" is the single most important thing you said. April Dunford is unambiguous on this: **you cannot position without knowing who your best customer is.** Full stop. You're trying to write the label before you know what's in the bottle.

(Ouch! That was brutal...)

>**Your instinct is right. Your proposed solution is wrong.**
>
 You've correctly diagnosed the problem: leading with "AI" is a category trap. It's noisy, vague, and it self-selects your audience down to people who already know they want AI — which is a fraction of the people who actually need what you do. Good diagnosis.
> 
But "AI-enabled technology and analytic consultancy" is not a position. It's a description. And it's a _mouthful_ of a description. Nobody wakes up and says "I need to hire an AI-enabled technology and analytic consultancy." That's not how buyers think or talk.

(Look at this AI trying to give me the performance review sandwich lol)

>**User:** But what about all the other tech consultancies? They don't lock into a particular industry or sector, they go after a range. This is a service business not a product business.

 > **Those consultancies you're thinking of? They earned the right to be generalists. You haven't yet.**
