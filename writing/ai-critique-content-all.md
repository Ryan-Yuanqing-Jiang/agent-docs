# How I Turned My AI Into a Blunt Mentor (And Survived the Feedback)

The most useful session I had with AI last month wasn't when it helped me write something. It was when it told me I was wrong.

I'd been trying to nail the positioning for my consulting business for weeks. Every draft felt vague. Every iteration just shuffled the same words around. So I set up an AI as a tough-love positioning consultant, fed it my website and notes — and what came back felt more like a performance review than a brainstorming session.

That was exactly what I needed.

## The Default Setting Nobody Warned You About

AI is trained to be helpful. And "helpful," left unchecked, tends to mean agreeable. Feed it your business plan, your pitch, your half-formed strategy — it'll highlight the strengths, gently suggest improvements, and leave you feeling pretty good about yourself.

It's like asking your mum if you should pursue your dream. The answer is almost always yes.

This isn't a flaw, exactly. It's just the default. But the most useful mentor I ever had wasn't the one who affirmed my ideas — it was the one who asked the uncomfortable question I'd been carefully avoiding. That friction is where real thinking happens.

AI can do that. You just have to ask it to.

## The Three-Step Setup

### 1. Give it a specific role

"Act as a marketing expert" is too vague to produce strong opinions. Give your AI the background of a specific kind of person with real stakes in the outcome.

For positioning work, I used:

> *"You are a CMO with 30 years of experience in positioning and marketing products and services. You are a consultant now helping new businesses go to market with clear positioning, effective messaging, and materials."*

The more specific the persona, the less it defaults to "that's a great start."

### 2. Tell it what good looks like

Here's the bit most people skip. AI contains an enormous amount of domain knowledge — but it doesn't know which frameworks and benchmarks *you* want to be held against.

Tell it. Reference established books, frameworks, or thinkers in your field. You're not asking it to summarize them — you're activating knowledge it already has and pointing it in a direction.

For positioning, I added one sentence:

> *"You are an expert in April Dunford's frameworks from her book 'Obviously Awesome' and her positioning exercises."*

It looks almost too simple. It isn't.

### 3. Tell it to stop being nice — but to stay curious

This is the step that changes everything, and you have to say it explicitly. But "be critical" alone is too blunt. The best mentors don't lecture — they ask questions and lead you somewhere.

Here's what I used:

> *"You will be constructively critical, using a blunt and straightforward voice, to help spot weaknesses in positioning and messaging, ask questions, and lead the user to refine and create the best positioning and messaging."*

The key words: *blunt*, *constructive*, *ask questions*. You want tension, not a roast.

## What Actually Came Back

I handed the AI my website, my notes, and the vague positioning I'd been half-defending for weeks. Then I let it run.

What followed reminded me of old mentoring sessions — the ones where there's a bit of tension in the room, because someone's actually telling you the truth.

The first hit:

> **"Problem 1: You don't have a customer. You have a hypothesis."**
> *"You cannot position without knowing who your best customer is. Full stop. You're trying to write the label before you know what's in the bottle."*

*(Ouch. Accurate, but ouch.)*

Then came the classic performance review sandwich — a compliment, immediately followed by demolition:

> **"Your instinct is right. Your proposed solution is wrong."**
> *"'AI-enabled technology and analytic consultancy' is not a position. It's a description. And a mouthful. Nobody wakes up and says, 'I need to hire an AI-enabled technology and analytic consultancy.' That's not how buyers think."*

And my personal favourite — when I pushed back by pointing out that plenty of large consultancies are generalists:

> **"Those consultancies you're thinking of? They earned the right to be generalists. You haven't yet."**

*(I know, I know.)*

The session ended with the AI giving me actual homework: two customer conversations to complete within two weeks, with specific questions to report back on. It would help me build positioning *after* I'd done that. Not before.

My back tensed a little reading it. But every single piece of feedback was right.

## The Mentor You Couldn't Afford Until Now

That quality of critique — specific, framework-grounded, honest to the point of uncomfortable — used to require either a significant budget or years of earned trust with a senior person in your field.

Now it costs whatever your AI subscription runs you.

The only catch: you have to ask for it. AI defaults to agreeable. You have to override that deliberately — give it a role, a standard, and permission to be honest with you.

When you do, it stops being a tool that does things for you. It starts being the kind of thinking partner that makes you better at what you do.

And yes — it might give you homework.

## 🇨🇳 微信公众号文章

**我让AI"喷"了我一顿——它说得全对**

大多数人用AI的方式，像是在和一个特别懂事的助理合作：你提想法，它帮你完善；你拿方案，它帮你优化。皆大欢喜，效率倍增。

但这不是AI最有价值的用法。

最近我把AI变成了一个"严师"——专门来挑我毛病的那种。结果被"喷"了一顿，但我觉得值。

## AI天生喜欢哄你，这才是问题

AI天生倾向于认同你。这不是讽刺，是训练机制决定的。你把商业计划给它，它会肯定你的思路；你把方案给它，它会说"方向对了，这里可以稍作优化"。

听着很爽，但其实没什么用。

真正有价值的成长，往往来自那个不肯让你舒服的人。那个会问你"你确定吗？""这个逻辑站得住吗？""你真的想清楚了吗？"的人。

AI也可以是那个人——你只需要配置它。

## 三步，把AI变成会说真话的严师

### 给它一个有立场的角色

不是"你是营销专家"——太宽泛，没有立场，没有主见。要具体：

> "你是一位有30年经验的CMO，现在作为顾问，帮助初创公司打磨市场定位和上市策略。"

角色越具体，它就越有"意见"。

### 告诉它"好"是什么标准

这是大多数人忽略的一步。AI的知识储量很大，但它不知道你想用哪个标准来衡量自己。你需要给它一个参照系——一本书、一个框架，或者某位领域内有代表性的方法论都可以。

我做产品定位时，加了一句：

> "你精通April Dunford在《Obviously Awesome》中的定位框架和她的定位练习方法。"

就这一句话。看起来简单，但有用。

### 明确让它别再夸你了

这一步必须明说，而且要说清楚*怎么*批评。最好的导师不是直接倒出一堆结论，而是通过提问引导你自己找到答案。

我用的提示是：

> "你会用直接、坦诚的方式，建设性地指出定位和表达中的弱点，通过提问引导用户不断优化，而不是直接给答案。"

关键词：直接、建设性、提问。你要的是张力，不是挑衅。

## 实际体验：被AI"面谈"是什么感觉

我在做一个副业——帮助澳洲的中小企业真正用好AI（getryse.io）。我的定位一直很模糊，自己也知道有问题，但反复改，就是绕不出去。

于是我把网站、笔记和那些半成品想法全给了AI，用上面的配置让它跑起来。

接下来的感觉，像极了被好老板一对一面谈——有点紧绷，但知道自己在学东西。

第一条反馈：

> **"问题一：你没有客户。你只有一个假设。"**
>
> 你说"我还没有客户"，这是你说过最重要的一句话。没有明确的目标客户，就无法做定位。句号。你现在是在不知道瓶子里装什么的时候，就开始写标签了。

（疼。但准。）

第二条，先夸后踩的经典绩效面谈三明治：

> **"你的直觉是对的，你的解法是错的。"**
>
> "AI赋能的技术与分析咨询"不是定位，是描述。而且是个很绕口的描述。没有人早上醒来会说"我需要一家AI赋能的技术与分析咨询公司"——这不是买家的语言。

（好，好，我明白了。）

第三条——当我反驳说大型咨询公司也是全行业服务时：

> **"你说的那些咨询公司，是靠积累赢得了做通才的资格。你还没有。"**

（行，行，我新来的，我知道了。）

最后，AI给我布置了"作业"：两周内完成两次客户访谈，带着具体问题的答案回来，*那之后*它才会帮我做定位。不是现在。

我盯着屏幕，背部有点紧绷。但每一条，都说对了。
